FRIDAY 4th JANUARY 1957

2d a day more

   We were working at something more like normal today. I found out at coffee-time that I was intended to do a N2 balance on Miss Morris, so I immediately had three estimations to do and also duplicate estimations on the first sample of Mr. Mason’s diet.

   I had a new washer-up start this morning, a Mrs Walters [Nora Walters] and she is also going to clean for us. It was intended that she wash up in the kitchen from 9.0 till 10.0am and 4.0 till 5.0pm, but Miss Beadle would not allow this as Nora would have been receiving 2d per day more than would be due to her. The Hospital pays 1d per hour less than the University! However, the new arrangement suits us better as I shall be able to supervise the maintenance of the lab. In the past we have not been able to tell Mrs Johnson exactly what we have wanted doing. Half the time she has not been free to come in to clean for us. Nora seems quite a hard-working type, reliable and pleasant. Maybe with two additions to the lab staff we shall now achieve the high standard of efficiency at which we are aiming, and which was marred in 1956 by Dennis and by the discovery that the Calcium method wasn’t too reliable.

   It was sad to hear this morning that Mr Barlow died at 7.45 last evening. He had a sub-arachnoid haemorrhage. I doubt whether he had been in the hospital for 3 hours when he died. He was only 22.

THURSDAY 10th JANUARY

A jewel robbery in Birmingham

   Tonight I went to the Men’s Fellowship, when we had a talk on Police Work in Birmingham by Inspector E. Brown. It was very well attended—there were about 24 present — and was a most entertaining evening. This was the second time Mr. Brown had addressed us, though I didn’t hear the first talk.

   Tonight’s talk was a series of personal reminiscences, some highly amusing, about some of the more notorious crimes in Birmingham in recent years. This was all thee more topical because over the weekend £18,000 worth of jewellery was stolen from a shop in Temple Street. It was one of the best planned crimes in Birmingham in years.

   Another story he told us was of seeing a man trying to wash blood from his clothes in a public lavatory. He arrested him; the man was charged with murder and subsequently tried and executed.

MONDAY 21st JANUARY

The Conscientious Objectors’ Tribunal

   I had to be at the Chamber of Commerce, New Street, by 10.15am when Mr Dibben and Alan Knott were meeting me. The Tribunal was held at 10.30am in Room 7 on the 2nd Floor, and I was the second person to be tried. I submitted eight references, from Cyril Plater, the Revd F.E.A. Shepherd, Mr F.J. Green, Ian Howard, George Cummings, Mr L.A. Bloomfield, Mr Moore and Dr. Gough. I was asked to read my own statement to the tribunal:—

   I believe that in our Lord Jesus Christ, God has revealed Himself to all mankind as a God of patience, pity, and love, which found perfect fulfilment in His death and resurrection.
   I accept Jesus Christ as my Saviour and He claims my love and loyalty. In His life and teaching, He has shown what we are meant to be. He has made clear that He is Lord of all men. Therefore, every Christian has a personal responsibility to his fellow men.
   Love and peace are the ultimate expression of this responsibility, and the consequence of all our thoughts, desires, and actions. Military preparation can only be a denial of this love and of Christ’s promise to fulfil this love in us.
   The question of pacifism has been described as the most difficult in personal philosophy. The pacifist must search his heart, shun self-deception and false idealism. Through constant prayer and in the Power of the Holy Spirit I have tried to do so. I have come to the conclusion that I can in no way take part in any form of military preparation. I therefore ask to be registered unconditionally as a conscientious objector.
   “Here stand I. I can no other. So help me God.”

   Afterwards I was asked some quite searching questions and at one point it became aggressive and Mr Dibben intervened. I knew that a lot of people were praying for me, but it was an ordeal nevertheless. I was quite prepared to go to prison if I had to. However, after some discussion they agreed to register me on condition that I remain in my work at the hospital for two more years, which I am happy to do.

   I called in at TI Exports in John Bright Street about 11.20am to see Freda and tell her the outcome. I met her from the train as usual [at Acocks Green Station, the 5.35pm from Snow Hill]. Tonight we went to the Olton to see The King and I with Yul Brynner and Deborah Kerr.

   Ann and Geoff got engaged yesterday.

MONDAY 11th FEBRUARY 1957

Birmingham is shaken by an earthquake

   I suppose the chief topic of conversation tonight is the great earth tremor which rocked much of the country at 3.45pm. I was sitting in the lab [on the first floor of Little Bromwich Hospital] when suddenly the whole building trembled and shook, and I got up expecting to see the walls crack and fall down. There was a lull for a second or two. Then a further tremor.

   It was an alarming experience. My first thought was that there had been an explosion. But there was no noise and it was an uncanny feeling. I met Freda off the train two hours later and at T.I.
