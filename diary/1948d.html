THURSDAY 4th NOVEMBER

   The Birmingham Mail has published the letter I wrote last week on “Schoolboys’ Pocket Money”:—

   Dear Sir

   I agree with your correspondent, Geoff Peters, in rejecting the idea that 2s. is enough pocket money for a teenage schoolboy. When our parents were young one could buy a quarter of a pound of sweets for approximately 2d., two oranges for 1d., ice-cream ½d. and 1d. and it cost 1d. to go to the cinema.

   Compare these prices with those of to-day. Sweets cost at least 6d., in fact anything up to one shilling per quarter. A visit to the cinema, with a good seat, is also one shilling; ice cream costs at least 3d.

   Supposing a boy receives two shillings a week, he either goes to a football match or cinema. Thus 1s. 3d. is spent, and by the time he has bought his quarter of sweets, he has little left of his two shillings. If he wants to buy any books for his hobbies, he needs at least one shilling more. Yours etc.,

      BRIAN D. WILLIAMS

      Acocks Green

[As the ink isn’t flowing very well and I have to “press on” to make it write, this is the last Diary-entry I shall make with the Fisherbond Pen-Pencil. I have used it on the “Addresses E–I” page to note three telephone numbers:—

Kerton, Dr. J.E., SPR 2210
Selly Oak Hospital, SEL 1361.

The letters are the first three letters of the district. SHI is Shirley, SPR is Springfield, and SEL is Selly Oak

   As no one in the road is on the telephone, I can only phone from the telephone box in the middle of the traffic island at the junction of Shirley Road, Olton Boulevard East and Wetherby Road. It costs 2d to make a call. One lifts the receiver, listens for the dialling tone, inserts two pennies in the box then dials the number. When the person answers, we press Button “A”; if there is no reply, Button “B”, then the money is returned. For long-distance calls we have to dial “0” for Operator and give them the number.

   The only people we know who are on the telephone are Mr. & Mrs. Moore at 54 Ulverley Green Road, Olton, Birmingham 27. Mr. Moore works for Premier Electric at Keeley Street, and also runs the Mission where we go on Sundays.]

TUESDAY 7th DECEMBER

   I collected the Cot-Fund as usual but it only came to 1/- as a lot of the boys did not want to give anything.

   I am learning to play Chess and have purchased the “Twin” Portland Chess & Draughts Board which, folded, measures 6 5/8 x 5¼ ins. and is portable. It is manufactured by Robinson & Sons Ltd., and comes from Chess, Sutton Coldfield, World Chess Specialists. One can use the board to play on on the ’bus, or to remember the positions in an unfinished game on a proper board. I played Donald Tipper at school today and won.

   I am learning about Chess from The Boy’s Companion, edited by B. Webster Smith, which I had for Christmas about three years ago. On the back of the board I have written in red ink the moves in the “Laird and Lady” game of Draughts. It is named after Laird and Lady Cambusnethan where the 5th move is 9–13. We play Draughts at home with a set of “Checkers” which I had as a present from the Americans when they came over in the war.

It is the worst report I have ever had. I have always been excellent at Maths. Last year Mr. Traynor said I was “very good indeed”, so why does Mr. Craig say I am “very weak”? If I still need to “master elementary mathematics” why was I placed in C2 and not C6?

   An odd thing happened. We recently had Logarithms for prep. but on our way to school on the Special Bus John and I found we had completely different answers. John asked how I had worked them out and, using my method, arrived at exactly the same answers. So when we handed in our prep it was identical.

   John is very good at Maths, so when Mr. Craig asked him how he had managed to get all the answers wrong, John explained the method he had used without (so far as I can remember) telling him it was my fault. Does Mr. Craig think that I sit next to John so that I can copy his work? Is that why he has such a low opinion of me?

   Looking through this Diary I see that I started using the “Addresses O–S” page to keep a list of the books I borrowed from Acocks Green Public Library. I have two tickets, one for fiction and one for non-fiction, and have two books out at a time, usually on pond-life, birds or astronomy etc. Unfortunately I have forgotten to record most of them. Those I have listed, together with the Library Number are:—


Sea Shore by Rev. Theodore Wood, J 591.92 W7
Observer’s Book of British Birds, by S. Vere Benson, J 598.2942 B35
Hero of Crampton School, by G. Forsyth Grant, J 48 G5
Chums at Last, by G. Forsyth Grant, J 48 G2
The Beresford Boys, by G. Forsyth Grant
Observer’s Book of British Wild Flowers

   I am not going to read any more of G. Forsyth Grant’s books, as they are all the same, very old-fashioned and sentimental, set in boys’ schools where one of the boys gets killed or loses an arm, resulting in two other boys who are bitter enemies becoming friends.

   [The books are very old. Mrs Forsyth Grant was born in Edinburgh in 1858 and died in 1926 aged 88. Her maiden name was Annie Gregory. The Boys at Penrohn was published in 1893 by W.P. Nimmo, Hay & Mitchell, who also published The Hero of Crampton School in 1895, and Burke’s Chum 1896; Chums at Last was published by Nelson & Sons in1899, and The Beresford Boys by W.P. Nimmo, Hay & Mitchell in 1906. Mrs Grant contributed to many journals, including Boy’s Own Paper and Vanity Fair.]
